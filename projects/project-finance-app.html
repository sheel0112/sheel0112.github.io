Stacked – Personal Finance & Budgeting App

Role: Product manager · Focus: smart budgeting, ML-driven categorisation, and low-friction UX.

Summary

  • Designed a personal finance tool aimed at young adults and couples who struggle to budget.
  • Built an experience that prioritises automation over manual spreadsheets.
  • Defined data contracts and flows that can scale to live banking integrations.

Context & problem

Most budgeting tools assume users will manually tag transactions and maintain intricate envelopes. In reality, many users want something closer to a “weekly financial pulse” that highlights what matters without demanding constant effort.

The goal for Stacked was to provide clear answers to three questions:

  • Where is my money actually going?
  • What is normal for me vs unusual?
  • What one or two actions should I take this month?

Constraints

  • Designed to integrate with open banking APIs (e.g. TrueLayer) with minimal assumptions.
  • Limited time from users – interface had to work even if they rarely adjusted settings.
  • Needed to be explainable: insights should be traceable back to underlying data.

Approach

Understanding behaviours

I mapped out common money patterns for the target audience: irregular income, shared expenses, subscriptions, and end-of-month stress. This informed core flows around recurring vs discretionary spend and shared budgeting.

Shaping the system

I defined the end-to-end system as:

  • Ingestion: connect accounts, pull transactions, normalise merchants.
  • Categorisation: rule-based layer plus ML suggestions with confidence scores.
  • Insights: monthly and weekly summaries, anomaly flags, and gentle nudges.
  • Presentation: responsive web UI with a simple “home” view and deeper drill-downs.

Framing delivery

To keep scope controlled, I shaped an MVP around:

  • 1–2 connected accounts.
  • Core categories (housing, food, transport, subscriptions, other).
  • One monthly email / summary view and a lightweight trends dashboard.

Outcomes

  • A clear product narrative that resonates with users who dislike “traditional budgeting”.
  • System design ready to plug into real transaction feeds and extend over time.
  • Reusable components for future work on shared accounts and couple-focused features.

What I’d explore next

The next stage would be deeper support for shared finances (households / couples) and improved explainability of ML decisions – making it obvious why certain transactions were flagged or categorised the way they were.
